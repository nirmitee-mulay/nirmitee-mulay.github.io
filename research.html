Research

Exploring the boundaries of consciousness through experimental and computational approaches

Research Overview

My research investigates fundamental questions about consciousness: What information can our brain process without awareness? How do we form judgments about our own mental states? And how can we leverage neuroscience to regulate emotional experiences? I approach these questions through a combination of rigorous psychophysical experiments, neuroimaging (fMRI), and increasingly, computational modeling.

With an interdisciplinary background spanning biology, physics, and mathematics from IISER Kolkata, I bring a unique perspective to consciousness research. My work emphasizes methodological rigor, response bias control, and open science practices—principles I promote through co-founding Sigma Sessions at BCBL, an initiative dedicated to replication, reproduction, and rigorous research.

Moving forward, I'm particularly interested in developing computational and theoretical frameworks—such as Bayesian models and predictive processing accounts—to better understand how conscious and unconscious processes interact in perception, working memory, and emotion regulation.

Current PhD Projects

🧠 Project 1: Unconscious Working Memory with Visual Ensembles

📅 2023 - Present 👥 Collaborators: Prof. David Soto, Dr. Cesar Caballero-Gaudes 🔬 Methods: Psychophysics, fMRI, MVPA

The Question: Can information be maintained in working memory without conscious awareness? This fundamental question has implications for our understanding of memory systems and consciousness itself.

The Challenge: Previous research suggesting unconscious working memory has been plagued by methodological issues, particularly response bias. When participants report information about stimuli they claim not to see, are they truly accessing unconscious memories, or are they simply guessing based on subtle biases?

Our Approach: We developed a bias-free paradigm using visual ensembles of faces. Instead of asking participants to report specific features of individual faces (which can introduce bias), we ask them to judge the average emotional expression of a set of faces. This ensemble coding approach, combined with rigorous psychophysical controls and continuous flash suppression (CFS) to render stimuli invisible, allows us to test unconscious working memory without the confounds of previous studies.

Key Findings: Our recent preprint challenges previous claims of unconscious working memory. When response bias is properly controlled for, we find no evidence that ensemble representations can be maintained without awareness. This highlights the critical importance of methodological rigor in consciousness research.

Impact: This work demonstrates how careful experimental design can overturn established findings and advance our understanding of the relationship between consciousness and memory. Our bias-free paradigm provides a template for future unconscious processing research.

👁️ Project 2: Metacognitive Assessment of Unconscious Face Ensembles

📅 2024 - Present 👥 Collaborators: Prof. David Soto 🔬 Methods: Psychophysics, Signal Detection Theory, Metacognition measures

The Question: When we process information without conscious awareness, can we form accurate metacognitive judgments about that processing? In other words, do we have any introspective access to our unconscious perceptions?

Why It Matters: Metacognition—our ability to monitor and evaluate our own cognitive processes—is often considered a hallmark of conscious awareness. Understanding whether metacognitive sensitivity exists for unconscious perceptions could reveal fundamental insights about the architecture of consciousness and the relationship between first-order perception and higher-order monitoring.

Our Approach: Building on the visual ensemble paradigm from Project 1, we investigate whether participants can make accurate confidence judgments about ensemble representations of faces rendered invisible through continuous flash suppression. We use sophisticated signal detection and metacognitive sensitivity measures (meta-d'/d') to distinguish genuine metacognitive access from guessing or bias.

Theoretical Implications: This project speaks to debates between higher-order theories of consciousness (which predict no metacognition without consciousness) and first-order theories (which might allow for some residual metacognitive sensitivity). The results will inform computational models of metacognition and consciousness.

💭 Project 3: Understanding and Regulating Emotion Through Decoded Neurofeedback

📅 2024 - Present 👥 Collaborators: Prof. David Soto, Dr. Cesar Caballero-Gaudes 🔬 Methods: Real-time fMRI, Multivariate pattern analysis, Neurofeedback

The Vision: What if we could give people real-time feedback about their emotional brain states, enabling them to learn to regulate their emotions more effectively? This project explores how decoded neurofeedback—a cutting-edge technique that uses machine learning to decode brain activity patterns in real-time—can enhance our understanding and regulation of emotional experience.

The Approach: We use multivariate pattern analysis (MVPA) to decode emotional states from fMRI brain activity, then provide participants with real-time feedback about these decoded states. This allows us to investigate both the neural mechanisms underlying emotional experience and whether people can learn to modulate their brain states through neurofeedback.

Scientific Questions: How are emotional experiences represented in distributed brain networks? Can people learn to regulate these representations through neurofeedback? What cognitive strategies do people spontaneously develop when given access to their emotional brain states? And critically, how does conscious awareness of emotional states interact with the ability to regulate them?

Clinical Potential: Beyond basic science, this work has implications for developing novel interventions for emotional dysregulation in conditions like anxiety, depression, and PTSD. Understanding the mechanisms through which neurofeedback works could inform more effective, personalized therapeutic approaches.

Computational Future: I'm interested in developing computational models of how emotional regulation occurs during neurofeedback—potentially leveraging predictive processing or reinforcement learning frameworks to understand the learning dynamics.

Past Research

Emotional Experience in Depersonalization

📅 Aug 2021 - May 2022 👥 Advisor: Prof. Naotsugu Tsuchiya (Monash University) 🎓 Master's Thesis, IISER Kolkata

Depersonalization (DP) is a dissociative condition where individuals experience a profound sense of unreality or detachment from their own thoughts, feelings, and body. Despite feeling emotionally "numb," individuals with DP often show normal physiological responses to emotional stimuli—a puzzling dissociation between subjective experience and objective measures.

In my master's thesis, I investigated the emotional experiences of individuals with DP traits, exploring whether the reported emotional blunting reflects genuine changes in emotional processing or alterations in introspective access to emotional states. This work laid the foundation for my ongoing interest in the relationship between neural processing and phenomenal experience.

Altered Visual Perception in Derealization

📅 May 2021 - Nov 2021 👥 Advisor: Prof. Naotsugu Tsuchiya (Monash University) 🔬 Research Project

Derealization (DR) is characterized by experiences of detachment from one's surroundings—the world appears "unreal," dreamlike, or visually distorted. Visual complaints are among the most common symptoms, with individuals reporting that the world looks "flat," "foggy," or lacking in color and depth.

This project investigated whether these subjective reports correspond to measurable changes in basic visual perception. Using psychophysical tasks, we assessed visual processing in individuals with DR traits, contributing to our understanding of how altered phenomenology relates to perceptual mechanisms.

📄 Publication: Kusztor, A., Mulay, N. N., Yamada, M., Hohwy, J., & Tsuchiya, N. (2023). Lifting the veil: Altered visual perception in derealisation.

Future Directions

As I look toward postdoctoral opportunities, I'm excited to deepen my expertise in computational modeling of consciousness and perception. I'm particularly interested in:

  • Bayesian models of perception and metacognition: How can probabilistic frameworks help us understand the computations underlying conscious and unconscious processing?
  • Predictive processing accounts of consciousness: Can predictive coding provide a unified framework for understanding perception, working memory, and emotional experience?
  • Neural network models: How can deep learning approaches inform our understanding of hierarchical processing in the brain?
  • Computational psychiatry: Can computational models of altered consciousness inform interventions for dissociative and emotional disorders?

I'm seeking postdoctoral positions in labs that value rigorous methodology, open science, and interdisciplinary collaboration—bridging experimental neuroscience, psychophysics, and computational approaches to consciousness.
